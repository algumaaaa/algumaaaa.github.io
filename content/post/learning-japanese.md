## A year of language learning!

Well, not quite. I took quite a long 6 month break due to university thesis writing taking over my life. Still, what a year! Wild to think that just ten months back I had only like 10 words of japanese vocabulary under my belt, now I'm at a whopping 600! Still not N5 level, but we're getting there.

I'm currently at a point where I can read some manga, some game dialog snippets, and even some anime here and there. I've been having a lot of fun with it these days so I figured i'd write about the journey so far.

### The Kanas

I actually learned Hiragana ages ago during the pandemic, but didn't do much of anything with it. It served me quite nicely when coming back into studying japanese however, as knowing that alphabet is the first big wall you'll face. I'm aware some learning methods try to advocate for Romanji instead for a while (I'm looking at you, silly green owl), but I really don't see the point. You won't see it anywhere in actual japanese.

The way I studied Hiragana back then was by using a combination of flash cards and a little notebook. What I would do is everyday i'd dedicate a half hour of my time to doing the entire hiragana deck followed by writing the whole alphabet into a notebook page. By the time that notebook was just about full, I was very comfortable with hira.

Nowadays however, with Katakana, I've added a bit more to my daily studies. Behold: the IEAIAIO method!

Bear with me here.

Basically, I'm still doing the whole Anki and notebook everyday stuff (Granted, i've dialed back the notebook a bit, but thats more on me being lazy with writing). However, on top of that, I've also been using this snipped of the famous System of a Down song [I-E-A-I-A-I-O](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eudOwe3Rz-0) that got stuck in my head during a particularly boring Psychology class and just writing it down in Katakana. I'd start with just vowels as in the song, then change it to, say, KI KE KA KI KA KI KO, and so on.

Why this works for me I'm not entirely sure, something about having to actually think of what you'll write toguether with the song helping me guide what I need to remember. It might not do anything for you, specially if you're not into that song, but hey i'm sure you can adapt this somewhat and get something out of it.


### Setting up Anki.

I figured i'd write this here in case anyone is still unfamiliar with this software.

Anki is just a flash card software that is a miracle worker for language learning. I've used it in other stuff too, but my daily use is focused towards japanese. In this section i'll walk you through setting up a basic Hiragana and Katakana deck.


#### 1 - Download the program
PCs:
https://apps.ankiweb.net/

ANDROID and IOS: 
Their respective stores, keep in mind the IOS version is paid. You can, however, just access the decks you already have setup through their ankiweb website.

#### 2 - Get a deck
[Here](https://ankiweb.net/shared/info/576084877) is a file for a basic deck that'll teach you hiragana and eventually katakana, I recommend you use that
Just download it and open the file, alternatively go into anki -> import deck

However, if you wanna be independent and go explore some decks for yourself, here's the [link](https://ankiweb.net/shared/decks):

Keep in mind you shouldn't flood yourself with decks, it'll be challenging to maintain even one at first, let alone multiple.

#### 3 - Configure your Anki

This step is optional, but I strongly recommend you do the stuff listed bellow:

- Click on the cog icon next to your installed deck to open the options for it
- Change the new cards per day to 10
- Change the maximum reviews per day to 9999
- Scroll down and enable FSRS
- Don't touch anything else

#### 4 - Using anki

- When you open your **hiragana** deck, you'll be shown the **front face of a card**, If you're following along this'll be あ
- What this software expects you to do is to mentally answer what that is, in this case, its the vowel A
- Once you answer mentally, click show answer, that'll show you the **back face of the card**
- If you got it right, click **Good**, if you got it wrong, click **Again**
- **DO NOT** use any of the other options, because reasons.
- Keep going until anki says you're done for the day
- Study your deck **EVERY DAY**, no skipping!

### Jo mako's japanese course

This is the meat of it.

What [Jo Mako's Japanese Course](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1IytlD4JjYO5-38wP6VbFNjHQDaDEsx4WWi2Rs-gaamk/edit?gid=159260467#gid=159260467) essentially is is a combination of an Anki deck of vocabulary that you do alongside a comprehensive list of reading material depending on far along the deck you are. You're 150 words in? You can now read the intro to Pokemon Fire Red! 185 words? Pokemon Heart Gold! And so on. And yes, there is a lot of pokemon on there.

The anki deck itself is just fine, but its the little carrot on a stick thing that is brilliant. Having a new media be available for you to read in your target language every 20-30 new words learned feels so motivating. The progress becomes palpable, measurable, show-off-able even!

This japanese course also has a list of anime, manga, and others with their respective difficulty and sometimes even anki decks of their own. This isn't anything new, [jpdb](https://jpdb.io/) is there after all, but again its the sum of its parts that make it so good.

### Immersion

The big popular word with language learning these days. For me personally it takes a backseat to the other stuff i've already listed, but I do try to do it every other day.

I personally quite enjoy reading Yotsuba as it has a really simple vocab along with just being very cute! I've also been spending some time with the texts over at [Tadoku](https://tadoku.org/japanese/), they're tailored for specific levels and are all quite comfy.

With watching stuff I used to do a lot of japanese gaming youtube, but with the slow realization that I did not want my japanese to be riddled with online slang I made the switch to anime. I've hopped around quite a bit, its difficult to pick something to watch that you care for but also not too much as you wont be understanding everything. Eventually I landed on Naruto Shippuden, mostly because of how popular it was here when I was a teenager.

### Song lyrics

Somewhere along this road I realised I badly needed an outlet of just writing stuff, lest this language become like my Spanish where I can read and listen just fine but can't write a sentence for the life of me. In comes this brilliant idea of just writing song lyrics from memory! Can you hazard a guess as to where this idea was adapted from? Yes, at the end of the day its just the IEAIAIO stuff but with extra steps, its been working wonderfully however!

The gist of it is just that I think of a song in a language i'm comfortable in - say, Yesterday by The Beatles - and I go through the lyrics, translating them into japanese the best I can. If I run into a word or sentence structure I'm not familiar with, I write a big question mark as a TO-DO marker for me to research later.

I imagine if you're more into other art forms you could easily adapt this little exercise. Keep in mind however, choosing a song with simpler vocabulary and less "poetic language" is naturally preferable, so I'd imagine adapting this into using poetry would be quite difficult.
